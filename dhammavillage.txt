ABOUT THE PROJECT
Core Concept
Dhamma Village is a structured rural community system designed to cultivate disciplined living, preventive mental well-being, and self-reliance through shared daily routines, collective responsibility, and sustainable resource management. Instead of short-term programs or external interventions, the model uses the living environment itself to shape stable behavior, cooperation, and long-term social balance.

The Project in Practice
The project involves constructing and operating a permanently functioning rural settlement. The village contains a central hall for daily group discipline activities, shared residential clusters, common kitchen and dining facilities, a first-aid health unit, a non-formal learning centre for children, skill development workshops, agricultural land, water management systems, and community activity spaces. Residents live and work within the system, share responsibilities, and maintain daily routines that support order, cooperation, and low-waste living.

Why This Model Exists
Rural communities increasingly face instability caused by unemployment, social fragmentation, family stress, and resource depletion. Conventional welfare programs often address symptoms after problems arise. The Dhamma Village model is designed as a preventive system. By structuring daily life, shared work, and resource use within a stable community environment, the project reduces dependency, conflict, and vulnerability while strengthening self-reliance and social cohesion.

Who It Serves
The project serves underprivileged rural families, individuals seeking stable living environments, youth requiring discipline and skill development, and children needing safe and structured growth spaces. Participation is inclusive and non-discriminatory. The village is not a commercial enterprise, religious institution, or residential school. It is a community development environment focused on long-term stability and well-being.

Closing Line
Dhamma Village is a replicable rural development model that combines environment design, shared responsibility, and sustainability to build self-reliant and harmonious communities.

HOW THE VILLAGE WORKS
Daily Routine Structure
Life in Dhamma Village follows a fixed daily schedule. Residents begin and end the day with quiet group practice in the central hall. Work, learning, meals, and rest follow defined time blocks. Children attend the learning centre during scheduled hours. Adults engage in farming, maintenance, kitchen duties, workshops, or community services. Routine is consistent across all residents to build discipline, stability, and cooperation.

Shared Facilities System
All essential services operate as shared community facilities. Housing clusters are arranged around the central zone. A common kitchen and dining hall provide daily meals. A first-aid health unit handles basic medical needs. A learning centre supports children’s development. Community toilets, water points, and bathing areas reduce waste and enable water reuse. A common activity ground is used for gatherings, sports, and events. Shared facilities reduce cost, duplication, and resource consumption.

Self-Sufficiency Systems
The village produces a significant portion of its daily needs internally. Agricultural land supplies food grains and vegetables. Livestock units support dairy, wool, and organic manure. Water harvesting and pond systems store and reuse water. Biomass and organic waste are converted into cooking fuel and compost. Workshops repair tools, furniture, and basic equipment. These systems minimize dependency on external supply chains and maintain low operating costs.

Governance and Collective Responsibility
The village does not operate under a single individual authority. Responsibilities are assigned through community consensus and trustee oversight. Daily duties rotate among residents. Maintenance, cleanliness, food preparation, and farming tasks are collectively managed. Decisions related to operations are discussed in community meetings. Trustees provide administrative compliance, financial accountability, and external coordination, while internal functioning relies on shared responsibility and cooperation.

Reality Statement
Dhamma Village is not a conceptual proposal alone. It is designed as a fully operational settlement model where infrastructure, daily routines, and governance systems work together to produce stable and self-reliant rural communities.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS (FAQ)
Is Dhamma Village a religious project?
No.
 Dhamma Village is not a religious institution, not affiliated with any religious or meditation organization, and does not conduct religious worship or rituals.
 It is a community development and rural self-reliance project operated by a public charitable trust.
 All activities are non-religious, non-political, and non-commercial.

Is this a school or residential school?
No.
 The village includes a non-formal community learning centre for children.
 It does not issue certificates, follow a formal syllabus, or replace formal schooling.
 It provides activity-based learning, life skills, and supervised developmental support.

Who can live in the village?
The village is designed for:
Underprivileged rural families


Individuals seeking stable and disciplined living environments


Youth requiring skill development and structured routines


Children accompanied by guardians


Participation is inclusive and non-discriminatory.
 The village is not a commercial housing project and does not operate on a landlord–tenant basis.

How is the project funded?
Funding is obtained through:
Donations to the Public Charitable Trust


CSR contributions


Government-linked development support


Institutional and philanthropic partnerships


Funds are used to build infrastructure and establish self-sufficiency systems.
 All funds are auditable and used only for project purposes.

Is the project government-approved?
The Trust is registered under applicable laws and maintains statutory compliance.
 All required registrations (Charity Commissioner, PAN, NGO DARPAN, Income Tax exemptions) are obtained or in process.
 Local administrative permissions are taken as needed for land use and community facilities.
The project is structured to align with rural development, sustainability, and community welfare frameworks recognized by government agencies.

Is this an ashram or meditation retreat?
No.
 Dhamma Village is a permanently operating rural settlement, not a retreat centre, course venue, or temporary residential camp.

Do residents receive salaries?
No.
 Residents participate in shared work and responsibilities.
 The model is based on collective contribution, not employment contracts.

Can visitors stay temporarily?
Yes.
 Visitors and collaborators may stay for defined periods under project guidelines, subject to capacity and operational schedules.

Is the model replicable elsewhere?
Yes.
 The project is designed as a modular and replicable rural community system.
 Once the pilot village stabilizes, it can be adopted in other regions through partnerships.

Who manages external compliance and accountability?
The Board of Trustees manages statutory compliance, financial accountability, and external coordination.
 Internal daily operations are managed through collective responsibility systems.
